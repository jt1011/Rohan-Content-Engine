# Pawel Huryn Content Intelligence Playbook

## Purpose

This document is a reusable writing brain for creating LinkedIn posts in the style of what performed best for Pawel Huryn from the analyzed CSV.

It is not a biography.
It is not generic LinkedIn advice.
It is a practical operating manual built from observed patterns in Pawel's posts.

Use this when you want to:
- write a post inspired by Pawel's winning structure
- understand why his content works
- generate stronger PM education posts
- build a SKILL file later for a broader content engine

## Source Base

Analyzed dataset:
- Creator: Pawel Huryn
- Dataset size: 560 LinkedIn posts
- Columns reviewed: post_text, likes, comments, shares, creator, date, and post format metadata where available

Posts were evaluated based on engagement signals from:
- likes
- comments
- shares

The goal was not to copy Pawel's wording.
The goal was to extract the repeatable logic behind what works.

# 1. Executive Summary

## The core truth

Pawel does not win because he sounds inspirational.

He wins because he packages product management education in a way that feels:
- sharp
- corrective
- visual
- practical
- easy to steal and reuse

His best posts usually do one of these things:
1. attack a bad PM assumption
2. explain a misunderstood PM concept
3. give a clean framework people can save
4. drop a free resource people instantly want
5. simplify AI for PMs into something practical

## The dominant formula

The highest-performing Pawel content usually follows this arc:

**Hard claim**  
→ **Name what people get wrong**  
→ **Break it down into steps, distinctions, or a mini-framework**  
→ **Explain it in plain language**  
→ **Add examples, tools, visuals, or a resource**  
→ **Close with a soft prompt or lead magnet CTA**

This is not story-led writing.

This is **teacher-led writing with strong opinions**.

# 2. What Actually Wins for Pawel

## A. Contrarian PM correction posts

These are some of the strongest performers.

They usually start by rejecting a common idea in product:
- "Product Owner is not a job title"
- "Most products are actually projects"
- "Product management is not about collecting requirements"
- "Bullshit management is spreading"

Why these work:
- they create tension fast
- they make experienced people nod
- they make newer PMs curious
- they position the writer as someone who can separate signal from nonsense

## B. Misunderstood PM concept breakdowns

Pawel performs well when he takes a concept people know by name, but not by depth:
- North Star Metric
- Product Discovery
- User Stories
- Product Vision
- Strategy vs Objectives vs Roadmap
- Outcomes vs Outputs
- Product vs Project

Why these work:
- they educate without sounding basic
- they help the audience feel smarter
- they create save-worthy posts
- they are perfect for diagrams, visuals, and framework slides

## C. Practical AI for PMs

Pawel's AI content works when it is concrete.

Good angles:
- how PMs should start learning AI
- how to use AI in product workflows
- agents and automation for PM work
- how AI changes documentation, planning, and execution
- prompt engineering or evals for PM use cases

Weak angle:
- generic AI hype
- vague future-of-work commentary
- "AI is changing everything" filler

Why the strong AI posts work:
- they reduce overwhelm
- they connect AI to daily PM tasks
- they make the reader feel they can act today

## D. Resource-led posts

Templates, maps, posters, frameworks, guides, downloads.

This is one of Pawel's biggest strengths.

He often turns educational content into:
- posters
- 101 guides
- learning maps
- cheat sheets
- downloadable PDFs
- visual explainers

Why these work:
- high save potential
- high share potential
- easy CTA
- strong value perception
- helps him build audience and demand without sounding salesy

## E. Authority from proprietary data or repeated teaching

When Pawel teaches using his own data or repeated exposure to PM problems, the post gets stronger.

Examples of high-trust setups:
- "16,000+ PMs completed my assessments"
- repeated callouts about the same PM confusion
- educational posts that sound like they came from seeing the same mistake many times

Why these work:
- he sounds earned, not performative
- the authority feels operational
- it suggests pattern recognition, not random opinion

# 3. Performance Signals and Structural Observations

## Format patterns

The strongest formats in the dataset were heavily visual.

Observed format trend:
- image posts performed strongest overall
- document posts were also strong
- video lagged behind image and document
- article format underperformed badly

### What this means
Pawel's writing is not separate from packaging.

His best content is often built to support:
- a diagram
- a chart
- a framework image
- a learning map
- a visual poster
- a downloadable asset

The caption matters.
But the asset often carries the distribution.

## Top post structure patterns

From the stronger-performing set:
- hooks were not ultra-short
- hooks were often around one clear idea, not a rambling intro
- captions were dense, but highly scannable
- paragraph count was high because he breaks thoughts aggressively
- bullets and numbered breakdowns show up often in strong posts

### Practical takeaway
Pawel is not writing "short posts."

He is writing **compressed educational posts** that are easy to scan.

That is a very different thing.

## Length observations from top performers

From the analyzed top-performing subset:
- average hook length was around 28 words
- average caption length was about 299 words
- paragraph count was high because ideas were broken into short units
- top posts frequently used bullet points or numbered lists

### Practical takeaway
Do not optimize for "short."
Optimize for:
- clarity
- scanability
- density
- save value

# 5. Hook Library

These are the hook families most aligned with Pawel's winners.

## Hook Family 1: Hard correction

Use when you want to reject a common assumption fast.

Templates:
- `[Term] is not [what people think it is].`
- `Most [professionals/teams/companies] get [concept] wrong.`
- `[Popular practice] is not [desired outcome].`
- `Too many [role/group] still confuse [A] with [B].`
- `Most [products/roadmaps/teams] are actually [less flattering truth].`

Examples:
- Product discovery is not customer interviewing.
- A roadmap is not a strategy.
- Most product teams are running projects, not products.

## Hook Family 2: Misunderstood concept

Use when teaching a familiar concept with new clarity.

Templates:
- `People talk about [concept] all the time, but few define it well.`
- `Everyone wants a better [concept]. Very few know how to design one.`
- `If your team struggles with [concept], this is usually why.`
- `The problem with most [concepts] is not execution. It is misunderstanding.`

Examples:
- Everyone wants a North Star Metric. Very few teams choose one correctly.
- The problem with most user stories is not the format. It is the thinking behind them.

## Hook Family 3: Strong distinction

Use when comparing two ideas that people blend together.

Templates:
- `[A] is not the same as [B].`
- `Confusing [A] with [B] creates bad [output].`
- `The difference between [A] and [B] matters more than most teams think.`
- `A lot of broken [systems/processes] start with mixing up [A] and [B].`

Examples:
- Vision is not strategy.
- Outputs are not outcomes.
- Product management is not requirements management.

## Hook Family 4: Anti-pattern warning

Use when naming bad behavior.

Templates:
- `One of the worst habits in [field] is [bad behavior].`
- `A lot of [teams/managers/PMs] are doing [bad behavior] and calling it [good thing].`
- `This is how bad [practice] spreads inside organizations.`
- `The fastest way to kill [desired outcome] is [anti-pattern].`

Examples:
- One of the worst habits in product is mistaking activity for progress.
- A lot of teams are shipping roadmap items and calling it strategy.

## Hook Family 5: Practical AI entry point

Use for AI content that stays grounded.

Templates:
- `PMs do not need to master all of AI to start using it well.`
- `The best way for PMs to learn AI is not what most people think.`
- `AI becomes useful for product teams when it connects to real workflows.`
- `Most AI advice for PMs is too vague to use.`

Examples:
- The best way for PMs to learn AI is to build one useful workflow.
- Most AI advice for product teams is too abstract to matter.

## Hook Family 6: Resource-first

Use when the post is built around a free asset.

Templates:
- `I created a [resource] to help [audience] understand [topic].`
- `Here is a simple [guide/map/framework] for [topic].`
- `If you are learning [topic], this [resource] will help.`
- `I turned [complex topic] into a one-page [resource].`

Examples:
- I created a simple product discovery map for PMs who feel lost.
- Here is a one-page guide to product vision, strategy, objectives, and roadmap.

# 6. Core Post Formula

This is the most reusable Pawel-style post skeleton.

## Master skeleton

### Step 1: Open with a strong claim
The first line should:
- reject
- clarify
- distinguish
- expose
- or simplify

It should not drift.

Good:
- Product Owner is not a job title.
- Most products are actually projects.
- Strategy is not a roadmap.

Weak:
- I have been thinking a lot lately about product.
- Here are some thoughts on strategy.
- Product management is a really interesting field.

### Step 2: Name the confusion
Immediately explain what people get wrong.

Examples:
- teams confuse delivery with product work
- PMs copy jargon without understanding the system behind it
- companies use role titles that hide responsibility gaps
- people talk about metrics without linking them to user value

### Step 3: Break the idea into parts
This is where Pawel shines.

Use:
- 3-part framework
- 4 distinctions
- 5 mistakes
- 6 steps
- ladder or hierarchy
- "A vs B vs C"

This gives shape to the teaching.

### Step 4: Explain in plain language
Each part should be simple and direct.

Not academic.
Not fluffy.
Not abstract.

Every section should answer:
- what it is
- why it matters
- what people miss

### Step 5: Add practical support
Use one or more:
- example
- diagram
- visual
- checklist
- resource
- use case
- common mistake
- diagnostic question

This is what turns content into value.

### Step 6: Close softly
Good ending styles:
- ask a relevant question
- invite debate lightly
- suggest a repost/share
- point to a PDF or guide
- continue the conversation without sounding needy

# 8. Formatting Rules

## Paragraph style

Pawel's posts are dense, but readable because they are broken up well.

Use:
- short paragraphs
- frequent line breaks
- one idea per block
- bullets when teaching multiple points
- numbered lists when sequence matters

Avoid:
- giant walls of text
- 8 ideas in one paragraph
- long storytelling intros before the point
- vague throat-clearing

## Recommended caption shape

Good range:
- 220 to 380 words for standard educational posts
- longer only when the post has real structure
- short is fine, but only if the idea is extremely sharp

## Bullet style

Best use cases:
- 3 to 6 points
- mistakes
- distinctions
- steps
- characteristics
- warning signs

## Scanability principles

Every post should be easy to skim for:
- the main thesis
- the framework
- the practical value
- the closing action

A reader should be able to get value in 10 seconds.

# 9. Closing and CTA Patterns

Pawel's closing style is usually soft and useful, not pushy.

## Best CTA styles

### 1. Discussion prompt
Use when the topic is debatable or diagnostic.

Examples:
- What would you add?
- What do you see teams get wrong most often?
- Which of these creates the most confusion in your company?

### 2. Resource CTA
Use when the post includes or points to an asset.

Examples:
- I turned this into a one-page guide.
- I created a poster for this.
- I made a downloadable framework around this topic.

### 3. Share/repost CTA
Use when the post is highly educational and broad.

Examples:
- Repost this so more PMs can find it.
- Share this with someone building a product team.
- Save this for your next strategy session.

### 4. Light invitation
Use when the goal is continued conversation, not hard conversion.

Examples:
- Happy to expand on this in the comments.
- Let me know which part you want me to unpack next.
- I can share the full framework if there is interest.

## Weak CTA patterns for this style

Avoid:
- overly emotional asks
- fake scarcity
- awkward "agree?" endings
- self-promotional pivots that break the teaching flow
- begging for engagement

# 12. What Makes the Best Posts Better Than the Average Ones

This is the most important section in the whole playbook.

The top posts are not just "good posts."

They do specific things the average posts do not.

## 1. They start with sharper tension
Top posts do not meander.

They start with:
- a claim
- a contradiction
- a correction
- a clean distinction

Average posts often start with context.
Top posts start with tension.

## 2. They teach one thing clearly
Average content can feel like a collection of thoughts.

Top content is usually about one core job:
- define something
- separate two things
- expose one anti-pattern
- share one framework

## 3. They are easier to save
Top posts often contain:
- a mental model
- a visual
- a list
- a map
- a guide
- a concept people want to revisit later

That makes them portable.

## 4. They sound more opinionated
Top content is more willing to say:
- this is wrong
- this is confusion
- this title is misleading
- this process is broken
- this distinction matters

Average content explains.
Top content judges, then explains.

## 5. They are built for packaging
This matters a lot.

The strongest Pawel content often feels like it could become:
- a slide
- a poster
- a framework image
- a PDF
- a teaching asset

That packaging mindset is a hidden growth engine.

# 13. Voice Rules

## Do

- sound like a product educator with strong pattern recognition
- be direct quickly
- teach through distinctions
- name common confusion without hedging too much
- use clear frameworks
- write in plain language
- make each post useful enough to save
- build around one core insight

## Do not

- sound inspirational just to sound warm
- overuse personal storytelling
- write generic career advice
- be abstract when the post needs structure
- stack buzzwords
- posture as a guru

- overcomplicate the language
- end with a needy CTA

Aim for this blend:
- 45% educator
- 25% operator
- 20% critic of bad product thinking
- 10% builder/resource creator
