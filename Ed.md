# 1. Executive Summary

## The core truth

Ed Biden does not win with vague product thought leadership.

He wins with three content engines:

1. **resource-heavy PM assets**
2. **sharp anti-dogma product takes**
3. **operator-grade leadership playbooks**

That is the center of gravity.

His best posts make the reader feel one of these things immediately:
- I can use this right now
- finally, someone said what I was thinking
- this is the clearest way I have seen this explained
- I need to save this for later
- this person has actually done the work

## The real formula

His strongest posts usually follow one of these shapes:

### Mode A: Free resource drop
- lead with a specific asset
- stack what is included
- explain why it matters
- make the examples feel real
- push one simple CTA

### Mode B: Contrarian product truth
- attack bad dogma
- explain why it fails in practice
- connect it to business reality
- end with a hard principle or operational lens

### Mode C: Executive playbook
- start with a role, scenario, or leadership problem
- break it into parts
- sound like an advisor, not a commentator
- give a framework that feels battle-tested

This is not a soft educational account.

It is a **high-utility product leadership account with strong opinions**.

# 4. What Separates the Best Posts from the Average Ones

This is the most important section in the playbook.

## 1. The winner posts make the value obvious immediately

Strong post:
- exact examples
- clear payoff
- obvious utility
- sharp thesis

Weak post:
- generic announcement
- indirect value
- "come join this thing"
- audience has to work to find the benefit

## 2. Specificity is one of the biggest growth levers

His best posts are unusually specific:
- 13x examples
- 16x examples
- 15x examples
- 6x roadmap types
- 4x decks
- exact role scenarios
- named assets like Miro template, Figma template, cheatsheet, toolkit

That matters.

This is not cosmetic packaging.
This is perceived usefulness.

## 3. He makes product thinking feel commercially credible

The biggest viral post in the dataset is not a framework post.
It is a credibility post.

Example:
- [Stop talking like a product manager](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7434897250386034688)

That tells you something important.

The deeper engine of the account is not just "teach PM methods."

It is:
**help PMs sound credible in a business context**

## 4. He attacks purity and ideology

Several winners say roughly the same thing in different ways:
- product purism is dangerous
- speed without clarity is dangerous
- fashionable frameworks are not enough
- product language can sound weak if detached from business reality

That repeated stance is a real brand asset.

## 5. The best posts feel transferable

People save them because they can use them:
- in interviews
- in strategy work
- with stakeholders
- with teams
- in leadership conversations
- in onboarding to bigger roles

## 6. The weak posts are usually too close to pure promotion

Low performers are often:

# 8. Formatting Rules

## Format priority

This is a visual and list-heavy account.

That means:
- the first line must carry the idea
- the asset often does a lot of the work
- bullets are part of the performance system
- readers should understand the value in seconds

## Paragraph rules

Use:
- short opening lines
- heavy line breaks
- bullets for included items, steps, benefits, examples, or truths
- clear section labels when needed

Avoid:
- long warm-up intros
- abstract reflection before payoff
- giant text walls
- complex metaphors

## Length guidance

Good working range:
- 220 to 360 words for most posts
- longer is fine when the post contains a real framework or asset
- shorter only if the hook and visual do almost all the work

## Bullet rule

Bullets matter a lot in this feed.

They are especially effective for:
- what is included
- benefits
- steps
- examples
- contrasts
- failure patterns
- role expectations

**Strong practical promise**  
→ **specific asset or sharp stance**  
→ **clear breakdown**  
→ **business-grounded explanation**

# 13. Voice Rules

## Do

- sound commercially literate
- make the value obvious fast
- use frameworks, examples, templates, and playbooks
- challenge PM dogma when it breaks in practice
- write like someone who has advised leaders, not just read PM books
- make business credibility a recurring theme
- be decisive
- teach in a structured way
- keep posts highly scannable

## Do not

- write vague product inspiration
- sound like a product purist
- lean on PM jargon without translating it
- make promo the entire point
- bury the benefit
- use soft generic leadership language
- post event announcements with no hard lesson
- overcomplicate the prose
