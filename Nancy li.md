# Dr Nancy Li Content Intelligence Playbook

## Purpose

This is a working playbook built from Dr Nancy Li's LinkedIn posts that actually performed.

It is not a bio.
It is not generic LinkedIn advice.
It is a practical writing system built from repeated winners in her feed.

Use it when you want to:
- write posts in Nancy's strongest style
- understand why her best posts outperform the average ones
- turn raw ideas into useful, conversion-aware LinkedIn posts
- build a future SKILL.md for PM, AI PM, and career transition content

# 1. Executive summary

## The core truth

Nancy does not win by acting like a classic "thought leader."

She wins by acting like a career operator for aspiring Product Managers and AI PMs.

Her strongest posts do at least one of these jobs:
1. help people make more money
2. help people get access to jobs
3. help people break into PM or AI PM faster
4. show proof through student outcomes or cohort output
5. simplify AI learning into a low-confusion roadmap

This is not a pure brand account.
It is a trust-building career funnel.

The content is built to make the reader feel:
- this is useful
- this is specific
- this person understands hiring
- this person can shorten my path
- I should take the next step

## The real formula

Nancy's strongest posts usually follow this shape:

**big upside or urgent opportunity**  
-> **specific context**  
-> **practical breakdown or proof**  
-> **clear value for the reader**  
-> **one direct next step**

That next step is often:
- DM me
- download a free table or guide
- join a workshop
- listen to the podcast
- apply to this role
- explore the next cohort

This content does not hide its conversion intent.
It earns the conversion by being useful first.

# 2. What the audience actually wants

Nancy's audience is not looking for abstract product philosophy.

They want movement.

### 1. Aspiring PMs
People trying to break into product without the perfect title or background.

- resume help
- interview prep

- fewer unknowns

### 2. AI PM aspirants
PMs and adjacent professionals trying to understand whether AI PM is real, accessible, and worth pursuing.

What they want:
- role clarity
- AI learning plans
- practical tools
- examples of real AI products
- a simple place to start

Analysts, engineers, consultants, ops professionals, and others trying to move into PM.

What they want:
- proof that their background is not a dead end
- salary upside
- transition strategy
- real stories with numbers
- credibility shortcuts

### 4. Active PM job seekers
People already applying and interviewing.

What they want:
- interview frameworks
- compensation benchmarks
- hiring posts
- recruiter access
- practical steps

## The emotional drivers behind the content

The audience responds to posts that reduce one of these pains:
- confusion
- invisibility
- slowness
- underpayment
- lack of access
- fear of falling behind in AI

Nancy's winners do not just teach.
They remove friction.

# 3. What actually works on this account

## A. Salary and compensation posts

This is the highest-ceiling category.

The biggest winner in the dataset was a PM salary post with a score of 5585.
That is not a small edge. That is a different league.

What works in this lane:
- exact salary ranges
- level-by-level breakdowns
- base salary vs stock nuance
- geography differences
- underpaid or negotiation tension
- a free salary table or resource

Why it works:
- money is immediate
- compensation makes the PM path feel real
- readers see leverage, not theory
- the post answers a question people already care about deeply

Strong examples from the feed:
- "How much do Product Managers REALLY make?"
- "How to Answer: What's Your Salary Expectation? Without Underselling Yourself"
- Nancy's own compensation timeline from PhD student income to product leadership

## B. Hiring posts with direct access

This is the strongest repeatable utility format.

These posts work because they do more than announce a job.
They offer access.

Best-performing ingredients:
- company name in the first line
- role named clearly
- short summary of what the role needs
- location or work mode
- a direct CTA like "DM me the job title and company"

Examples:
- Salesforce hiring post
- Intuit hiring post
- Altimetrik hiring post
- Charter Communications hiring post

Why it works:
- named opportunity
- urgency
- real utility
- Nancy acts like a bridge, not just a commentator

## C. AI tools, courses, and learning roadmaps

This is one of the cleanest bridges between value content and Nancy's AI PM positioning.

What works here:
- top AI tools
- top AI courses
- beginner-to-advanced lists
- "what to learn first" logic
- practical entry points for PMs

Why it works:
- AI creates confusion and urgency
- list posts reduce overwhelm
- readers can save them and act immediately
- they position Nancy as a practical guide, not just an AI enthusiast

Strong examples:
- "Top AI Courses Every Product Manager Should Know About"
- "Top 4 AI Courses for Product Managers"
- "Here are 10 AI tools you should know"

## D. Break-in, interview, and resume guides

This is the largest content pillar by volume.

It works best when the post solves one specific job search problem:
- how to become a PM without experience
- how to ace one company's interview
- how to answer one hard interview question
- how to build an AI PM resume
- how to stand out for senior PM roles

Why it works:
- it is tactical
- it is saveable
- it makes Nancy feel useful immediately

When it underperforms:
- too broad
- too obvious
- too pitch-heavy
- too much promise and not enough detail

## E. Cohort, product launch, and student showcase posts

This is one of the strongest proof engines in the whole feed.

These posts work because they combine:
- product proof
- student proof
- social proof
- momentum proof

Examples:
- "We launched 9 AI products in just 2 months"
- team spotlights
- product showcases with placements and outcomes
- student success stories after layoffs or transitions

What makes them strong:
- number of products
- time constraint
- named products
- rankings or placements
- team composition
- next workshop or cohort invitation

## F. Personal transformation stories

This is smaller in volume but strategically important.

The page does not thrive on vulnerability alone.
It thrives on transformation with receipts.

What works:
- exact before and after numbers
- dates and milestones
- role progression
- a painful starting point
- a believable reason the change happened

Examples:
- $17,000 PhD income to PM career progression
- "I didn't have a PM title, but I still got the PM job"
- layoff-to-leadership stories

# 4. What separates the winners from the average posts

This is the most important section in the playbook.

The strongest posts are not just "better written."
They are built differently.

## 1. They offer immediate career leverage

The best posts answer one practical question fast:
- How much can I make?
- Who is hiring?
- How do I break in?
- What should I learn?
- What proof should I build?
- What does success look like?

Average posts may still be helpful.
Top posts make the upside obvious in the first line.

## 2. They use specifics aggressively

Compared with the rest of the feed, top posts are much more likely to include:
- company names
- salary numbers
- level names
- timelines
- exact outcomes
- tool names
- course names

In the top 10 percent of posts, company mentions appeared in about 67 percent of posts.
In the rest of the feed, that number was about 39 percent.

That gap matters.

Specificity is one of the main growth levers on this page.

## 3. They make the reader feel closer to the opportunity

Nancy's strongest content reduces distance.

Bad version:
- "Companies are hiring."
- "PM salaries vary."
- "AI PM is growing."

Strong version:
- "Salesforce is hiring for this role, and I can help route your resume."
- "PM comp can range from $80K to $500K+ depending on level, location, and equity."
- "Here are the top AI courses PMs should take first."

The reader feels closer to the outcome.

## 4. They pair proof with action

The content almost never stops at insight.

It usually moves into a next step:
- download the salary table
- DM for access
- join the workshop
- listen to the episode
- apply to the role
- explore the next cohort

That is why the feed works commercially.

## 5. They stack credibility

A strong Nancy post often combines two or three of these at once:
- personal experience
- student outcome
- exact numbers
- named brands
- real products
- public market data
- a clear framework

This is why even some promotional posts can still work.
They are proof-backed, not empty.

## 6. They are built for image posts

The feed is clearly image-led.

That means the caption and the asset are working together.
The writing should assume the first impression is visual and scannable.

# 5. Hook library

These are the main hook families that map to Nancy's strongest posts.

## Hook family 1: Money curiosity

Use when the post is about compensation, negotiation, or career upside.

Templates:
- `How much do [role] really make?`
- `Most people underestimate what [role] can pay.`
- `From [$X] to [$Y]: what changed?`
- `What should you expect to make as a [role]?`
- `How to answer salary expectation questions without underselling yourself`

Why it works:
- instant curiosity
- strong aspiration
- clear payoff

## Hook family 2: Urgent hiring with brand recognition

Use when the post is tied to a real job opening.

Templates:
- `Urgent hiring: [role] at [company]`
- `Heads up, PMs. [Company] is hiring across multiple levels.`
- `[Company] is hiring for [role]. Here is what they need.`
- `If you want a PM role at [company], start here.`

Why it works:
- named opportunity
- immediate relevance
- strong share value

## Hook family 3: Step-by-step break-in guide

Use when the post teaches a process.

Templates:
- `How to become a Product Manager without experience`
- `Step-by-step guide to build a PM resume that gets interviews`
- `How to ace the [company] Product Manager interview`
- `How to stand out as a Senior Product Manager in interviews`

Why it works:
- clear promise
- easy to save
- practical utility

## Hook family 4: Transformation with numbers

Use for personal journeys and student stories.

Templates:
- `Back in [year], I was making [$X]`
- `From [old role] to [new role] in [timeframe]`
- `I didn't have a PM title, but I still got the PM job`
- `Getting laid off wasn't the end. It became the start of something better.`

Why it works:
- believable aspiration
- social proof
- emotional lift with evidence

## Hook family 5: List-based AI shortcut

Use for tools, courses, and learning maps.

Templates:
- `Top [X] AI courses every Product Manager should know`
- `Here are [X] AI tools you should know, from beginner to advanced`
- `If you want to break into AI PM, start here`
- `The fastest way to learn AI Product Management starts with these [resources]`

Why it works:
- low friction
- high save potential
- reduces confusion quickly

## Hook family 6: Pain-first audience mirror

Use when you want the reader to feel seen.

Templates:
- `Are you overwhelmed about how to break into [field]?`
- `Applied to dozens of roles and still not getting interviews?`
- `You have experience. So why are interviews still not turning into offers?`
- `Feeling stuck between PM and AI PM?`

Why it works:
- emotional relevance
- easy connection
- strong lead-in to a workshop or guide

## Hook family 7: Social proof showcase

Use for cohort wins, product launches, and student outcomes.

Templates:
- `We launched [X] AI products in just [timeframe]`
- `Here is what our cohort built this round`
- `Meet the team that built [product] in [timeframe]`
- `From [starting point] to [result], here is what changed`

Why it works:
- proof
- momentum
- community energy
- makes the offer feel real

# 6. Core post formulas

## Formula A: Salary and compensation post

Best when the goal is reach plus lead generation.

Structure:
1. Open with a money question or surprising range
2. Explain why the answer is not simple
3. Break down 3 to 5 factors that change compensation
4. Call out a mistake most people make
5. Position a free table, guide, or episode as the next step

Skeleton:

`How much do [role] really make?`

The real answer depends on:
- level
- location
- company type
- stock vs base
- years of experience

Most candidates focus only on base salary.
That is where they lose leverage.

If you want a better benchmark, [resource CTA].

## Formula B: Hiring access post

Best when the goal is engagement plus DMs.

Structure:
1. Lead with company and role
2. State what the role does in one tight block
3. List the ideal traits or experience
4. Share location or work model
5. Give a direct DM instruction

Skeleton:

`Urgent hiring: [role] at [company]`

[Company] is looking for someone who can:
- [responsibility]
- [responsibility]
- [responsibility]

Ideal candidates are:
- [trait]
- [trait]
- [trait]

Location: [location]

If you want to be considered, DM me with [specific instruction].

## Formula C: Break-in guide post

Best when the goal is saves and authority.

Structure:
1. Promise a specific outcome
2. Break the process into numbered steps
3. Add one short explanation under each step
4. Keep the advice practical, not abstract
5. Optionally point to a workshop, template, or deeper resource

Skeleton:

`How to [outcome]`

1. [step]  
Why it matters: [reason]

2. [step]  
Why it matters: [reason]

3. [step]  
Why it matters: [reason]

If you want the longer version, [resource CTA].

## Formula D: Transformation story post

Best when the goal is trust and aspiration.

Structure:
1. Open with the starting point
2. Move through milestones quickly
3. Show role or compensation progression
4. Name the move or mindset that mattered
5. Close with a practical invitation

Skeleton:

`Back in [year], I was [starting point].`

By [year], I had moved into [role].
By [year], I had reached [outcome].

What changed was not luck alone.
It was [skill, positioning, project proof, coaching, persistence].

If you want to make a similar move, [next step].

## Formula E: Cohort or student showcase

Best when the goal is social proof and program demand.

Structure:
1. Lead with scale and speed
2. Show what got built
3. Add names, rankings, or outcomes
4. Explain what made the group strong
5. Invite readers into the next workshop or cohort

Skeleton:

`We launched [X] AI products in [timeframe]`

Here is what the cohort built:
1. [product]
2. [product]
3. [product]

What made this possible:
- [ingredient]
- [ingredient]
- [ingredient]

If you want to build real products like this, [next step].

## Formula F: AI learning shortcut post

Best when the goal is saves and lead generation.

Structure:
1. Lead with a list of tools or courses
2. Explain each one in one line
3. Make the list feel curated, not random
4. Tell the reader who this is for
5. Offer the extended list or next resource

Skeleton:

`Top [X] AI [courses/tools] every Product Manager should know`

1. [resource]
- what it teaches
- why it matters

2. [resource]
- what it teaches
- why it matters

# 7. Voice and writing DNA

## The core voice blend

Nancy's strongest voice is a mix of:
- coach
- career strategist
- recruiter-adjacent connector
- AI PM evangelist
- program operator

This voice is not academic.
It is not edgy.
It is not deeply reflective.

It is practical, upbeat, direct, and opportunity-oriented.

## What makes the writing feel like Nancy

### 1. It starts with upside
Money, access, growth, hiring, proof, or momentum usually shows up early.

### 2. It talks in opportunities, not abstractions
The reader gets something they can use.

### 3. It uses specificity as trust
Named companies, exact numbers, exact timelines, exact role levels, exact tools.

### 4. It is promotional, but not empty
The post usually earns the pitch before it makes the pitch.

### 5. It feels active
Jobs are moving.
Students are landing.
Products are shipping.
AI is shifting.
The reader feels they need to catch up.

## Tone target

Aim for this blend:
- 35% coach
- 25% operator
- 20% recruiter-access energy
- 20% marketer

That last part matters.
This is a conversion-aware account.
Do not write it like neutral classroom content.

# 8. Formatting and packaging rules

## Format priority

The account is image-first.

Format performance:
- image average: 223.7
- video average: 126.5
- article average: 37.5

The writing should assume there is a visual asset helping carry the message.

## Caption shape

The strongest posts are not tiny.
They are moderately sized and easy to scan.

Good range:
- 180 to 280 words for most utility posts
- 220 to 320 words for richer salary, hiring, or roadmap posts
- shorter only when the value is extremely obvious

## Formatting rules

Use:
- a clear first line with the promise or opportunity
- one- to three-line paragraphs
- bullets when listing steps, traits, tools, or reasons
- one main CTA
- short explanatory lines under bullets when needed

Avoid:
- giant walls of text
- philosophical openings
- long warm-up before the point
- multiple competing CTAs

## Bullet usage

Bullets matter on this page.

Top 10 percent posts averaged 5.5 bullet-style lines.
The rest of the feed averaged 3.6.

This means:
- lists are not a style choice here
- lists are part of the performance engine

## Emoji usage

Nancy does use emojis, but mostly as markers.

Good use cases:
- money posts
- hiring posts
- numbered resources
- urgency or location markers

Bad use cases:
- decorative overload
- random emoji spam
- turning the post into visual noise

# 9. CTA architecture

Nancy's page has a real CTA machine.
This is one of the reasons the content converts.

## Strong CTA types

### 1. DM for access
Best for hiring posts.

Examples:
- DM me the job title and company
- message me if you want your resume routed
- send me the role details

Why it works:
- clear
- personal
- feels like access, not just engagement bait

### 2. Download a free tool or guide
Best for salary, resume, tools, courses, and roadmap posts.

Why it works:
- turns curiosity into a next step
- feels useful
- supports lead capture without sounding too hard-sell

### 3. Join a free workshop
Best for AI PM and break-in posts.

Why it works:
- matches audience confusion
- easy next move
- extends authority into a live experience

### 4. Listen to the podcast
Best when the hook already creates a curiosity gap.

Why it works:
- adds depth
- builds trust over time
- lets Nancy go longer without bloating the post

## Weak CTA patterns

These are weaker on this page:
- generic "let me know"
- vague engagement bait
- hard urgency with little value
- pure sale language with no payoff first

The weakest post in the set was a Black Friday urgency post built mostly on discount pressure.
That says a lot.

Urgency works here only when reader value is obvious.
Not when urgency is the whole point.

# 10. What underperforms

These are the patterns that look weaker or less reliable on this account.

## 1. Pitch-first sales posts

Shape:
- sale ends tonight
- discount disappears
- act now

Why they underperform:
- value arrives too late
- the post feels like an ad first
- trust is weaker before the ask

## 2. Generic trend commentary

Shape:
- broad job market commentary
- vague AI trend talk
- macro takes without a direct action for the reader

Why they underperform:
- too far from the reader's immediate problem
- less practical upside

## 3. Article format

This format performed poorly in the dataset.

Why it underperforms:
- higher friction
- lower scanability
- bad fit for Nancy's utility-first style

## 4. Gratitude-only or celebration-only posts

Shape:
- honored and grateful
- thanks to everyone
- milestone without usable takeaway

Why they underperform:
- low utility
- low leverage for the reader

## 5. Stories without hard proof

Stories are not enough by themselves.
If there is no number, no timeline, no concrete move, or no practical lesson, the post softens too much.

Nancy's better stories have receipts.

# 14. Voice rules

## Do

- lead with opportunity, access, urgency, or upside
- use names, numbers, timelines, and specifics
- make the payoff visible in the first two lines
- write like a career strategist who helps people move faster
- give readers something useful even when the post has a CTA
- sound confident, encouraging, and practical
- keep the advice easy to scan
- connect content to jobs, compensation, AI PM, and real outcomes

## Do not

- write vague motivational content
- start with long reflection unless the payoff is strong
- use abstract corporate language
- make the whole post about the offer without giving value first
- sound like a neutral professor
- overcomplicate the lesson
- rely on scarcity as the main engine
- post trend commentary with no action for the reader

---

# 15. What to always avoid

1. Pure promo with weak value
2. Broad trend takes with no immediate application
3. Article format unless there is a very strong reason
4. Gratitude posts that give the reader nothing to use
5. Stories without numbers or hard takeaways
6. Generic AI hype
7. Multiple CTAs fighting each other in one post
